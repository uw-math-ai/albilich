# Root synthesis via the maximal-subgroup interface

## Target and permitted premises
Fix distinct primes p and q. We prove that there is a finite group A_m which embeds in no finite group invariably generated by elements of orders p and q. The argument uses exactly the two current root premises: the integrated minimal-host reduction `claim_minimal_host_monolithic_wreath_reduction_rev107` and the verified terminal simple-factor exclusion `claim_terminal_simple_factor_exclusion_rev128`.

## Lemma 1: maximal-subgroup criterion for two-element invariable generation
Let G be finite and let a,b be in G. Then a and b invariably generate G if and only if there is no maximal subgroup M<G such that both conjugacy classes a^G and b^G meet M.

Proof. If a^g and b^h lie in a maximal subgroup M, then the subgroup they generate is contained in M and is proper, so a,b do not invariably generate G. Conversely, if they do not invariably generate G, choose g,h with L=<a^g,b^h><G. Finiteness places L in a maximal subgroup M, and then a^G and b^G both meet M. This proves the equivalence.

## Lemma 2: the minimal-host interface
Suppose A_m embeds in a finite group invariably generated by elements x and y of orders p and q, and choose such a host G of minimum order. The integrated minimal-host premise gives a unique nonabelian minimal normal subgroup N=S^t, identifies

S^t <= G <= Aut(S) wr Sym(t),

and gives an injective coordinate projection A_m -> S. Thus S is a nonabelian finite simple group containing a copy of A_m, and G itself is an interval to which the terminal premise applies.

## Lemma 3: the terminal common-overgroup interface
Choose m as supplied by `claim_terminal_simple_factor_exclusion_rev128`. For every nonabelian finite simple S containing A_m, every t>=1, every X with S^t<=X<=Aut(S) wr Sym(t), and every pair of elements of X having orders p and q, there are independently chosen u,v in S^t and one proper subgroup D<X such that the conjugates of the two elements by u and v both lie in D.

## Root theorem
For this m, suppose for contradiction that A_m embeds in a finite group invariably generated by x and y of respective orders p and q. Choose an order-minimal host G. Lemma 2 supplies N=S^t<=G and an embedding A_m<=S. Apply Lemma 3 with X=G. There exist u,v in S^t and a proper subgroup D<G such that x^u and y^v lie in D. Since S^t<=G, the elements u and v are allowable conjugators in the definition of invariable generation. But

<x^u,y^v> <= D < G,

contradicting that x and y invariably generate G. Equivalently, extending D to a maximal subgroup and invoking Lemma 1 gives the same contradiction. Hence no such host exists. Since A_m is finite, the assertion that every finite group has such a host is false; the answer to Kourovka Problem 21.142 is negative.

## Four-step proof spine
1. Maximal-subgroup criterion: failure of invariable generation is exactly simultaneous intersection of the two conjugacy classes with one maximal subgroup.
2. Minimal-host reduction: any hypothetical host yields S^t<=G<=Aut(S) wr Sym(t) with A_m embedded in S.
3. Terminal factor exclusion: the chosen m forces every such S into a verified family whose p- and q-elements have independent S^t-conjugates in one common proper subgroup.
4. Assembly: Steps 2 and 3 produce the forbidden subgroup from Step 1, so the hypothetical host cannot exist.

Dependency arrows: Step 2 + Step 3 -> common proper subgroup in G; common proper subgroup + Step 1 -> failure of invariable generation; failure for every hypothetical host -> negative answer to the root question. No theorem-level gap remains in this inference once the two named premises are accepted.

## Representation switch and portfolio synthesis
The direct representation is the universal statement <x^g,y^h>=G for all g,h in G. The maximal-subgroup representation replaces it by a conjugacy-class incidence condition. The structural representation then replaces an arbitrary hypothetical host by its order-minimal monolithic witness S^t<=G<=Aut(S) wr Sym(t). The terminal family modules speak exactly in that structural representation, while Lemma 1 translates their common proper subgroup back to the negation of the original universal statement. The source-based CFSG/projective-degree work and the classical-family constructions are therefore closed premise modules; the previous orthogonal adversarial probes found no surviving mismatch, so reopening their intrinsic-recognition precursors would not address the live root inference.

## Independent proof attacks
Attack A, direct quantifier attack: Lemmas 2 and 3 immediately give allowed conjugators u,v in G for which the generated subgroup is proper. This closes the target directly.

Attack B, maximal-subgroup attack: extend the common proper subgroup D to a maximal M. Then x^G and y^G both meet M, and Lemma 1 contradicts invariable generation. This independently checks that neither the subgroup nor conjugator quantifiers were reversed.

## Strongest failed route
Trying to classify or control a completely arbitrary host before minimizing fails because there need not be a canonical simple factor carrying A_m; the integrated minimal-host lemma removes exactly that obstruction, so the arbitrary-host route is abandoned rather than retried.
