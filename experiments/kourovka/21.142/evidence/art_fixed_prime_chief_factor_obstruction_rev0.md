# Proof dossier

Fix distinct primes \(p,q\), and put \(d=pq\). The strongest route found in this pass is negative: prescribed prime orders force all sufficiently large alternating chief factors into a common product-type subgroup. A parallel linear-algebra argument gives the same conclusion for high-dimensional projective special linear chief factors when the induced automorphisms are inner-diagonal. Thus a counterexample of the form \(A_m\) will follow once the corresponding common-overgroup lemma is completed for the remaining classical automorphisms and form-preserving families.

## 1. Two equivalent representations of invariable generation

For elements \(x,y\in H\), the following are equivalent.

1. \(x,y\) invariably generate \(H\).
2. There is no proper subgroup \(M<H\) meeting both conjugacy classes \(x^H\) and \(y^H\).
3. In every nontrivial transitive action of \(H\), at least one of \(x,y\) is a derangement.

Indeed, conjugates \(x^g,y^h\) fail to generate precisely when they lie in a common maximal subgroup. In the coset action on \(H/M\), an element has a fixed point precisely when its conjugacy class meets \(M\). One immediate necessary condition is that every proper subgroup of \(H\) has index divisible by \(p\) or by \(q\): if \([H:M]\) is divisible by neither prime, every permutation of order \(p\) or \(q\) on \(H/M\) has a fixed point.

The maximal-subgroup representation is effective for individual almost simple quotients. For embedding questions, however, the chief-factor representation is stronger: if a chief factor is \(S^t\), the conjugation action embeds the corresponding quotient between \(S^t\) and \(\operatorname{Aut}(S)\wr S_t\), and independent conjugation by \(S^t\) becomes a coordinatewise gauge transformation.

## 2. Failure of the direct alternating-host attack

### Lemma 2.1 (common invariant subset)

Let \(r\in\{p,q\}\), let \(n>d\), and let \(z\in S_n\) have order dividing \(r\). Then \(z\) stabilizes a subset of size \(d\).

### Proof

If \(z=1\), the assertion is immediate. Otherwise its cycles have lengths \(1\) and \(r\). Write \(c\) for the number of \(r\)-cycles and \(f=n-rc\) for the number of fixed points. If \(c\ge d/r\), take the union of \(d/r\) nontrivial cycles. If \(c<d/r\), take all \(c\) nontrivial cycles and \(d-rc\) fixed points; there are enough because
\[
f=n-rc>d-rc.
\]
This gives a \(z\)-invariant \(d\)-set. \(\square\)

Consequently, for \(n>pq\), no pair of permutations of orders \(p,q\) invariably generates either \(A_n\) or \(S_n\): independently conjugate their invariant \(d\)-sets to the same fixed set \(\Delta\), after which both elements lie in the proper set stabilizer
\[
K=(S_d\times S_{n-d})\le S_n.
\]
The strict inequality is essential. At \(n=pq\), the construction may produce only the whole underlying set.

## 3. Alternating chief factors are excluded even with multiplicity

### Theorem 3.1 (alternating chief-factor obstruction)

Let \(n>pq\). If a finite group \(H\) is invariably generated by elements of orders \(p\) and \(q\), then no chief factor of \(H\) is isomorphic to \(A_n^t\) for any \(t\ge1\).

### Proof

Assume that, after quotienting below a chief factor, \(N=A_n^t\) is a minimal normal subgroup. Factor further by \(C_H(N)\). The resulting group \(X\) is a quotient of \(H\), hence is invariably generated by the images \(a,b\) of the original generators. Moreover,
\[
N\le X\le \operatorname{Aut}(A_n)\wr S_t=S_n\wr S_t,
\]
because \(n>pq\ge6\) implies \(n\ge7\). Neither \(a\) nor \(b\) is trivial: otherwise the noncyclic group \(X\), which contains \(N\), would be generated by one element. Thus \(|a|=p\) and \(|b|=q\).

Let \(K=S_d\times S_{n-d}\) be the stabilizer of a fixed \(d\)-set \(\Delta\), and put \(W_K=K\wr S_t\). We claim that every element \(z\in S_n\wr S_t\) of order \(r\in\{p,q\}\) is conjugate by an element of \(A_n^t\) into \(W_K\).

Write \(z=(g_1,\ldots,g_t)\sigma\). Every cycle of \(\sigma\) has length \(1\) or \(r\). On an \(r\)-cycle of factor indices, the product of its edge labels is the identity because \(z^r=1\). Hence the labels form a coboundary and conjugation by a suitable element of \(S_n^t\) makes all labels on that cycle trivial. This conjugator can be replaced by one in \(A_n^t\) while keeping the resulting labels in \(K\): if the original coordinate conjugators are \(u_i\in S_n\), choose \(k_i\in K\) with the same parity as \(u_i\), and replace \(u_i\) by \(u_i k_i^{-1}\in A_n\). Relative to the fully trivialized labels, this replacement introduces only products of the \(k_i\), hence labels in \(K\). Such \(k_i\) exist because \(K\) contains both even and odd permutations.

On a fixed factor of \(\sigma\), the corresponding label has order dividing \(r\). Lemma 2.1 supplies an invariant \(d\)-set. Since \(A_n\) is transitive on \(d\)-subsets, an \(A_n\)-conjugate of that label lies in \(K\). Treating all factor cycles independently proves the claim.

Choose \(u,v\in N\) so that \(a^u,b^v\in W_K\). Then
\[
\langle a^u,b^v\rangle\le X\cap W_K<X,
\]
where properness follows from \(N=A_n^t\not\le W_K\). This contradicts invariable generation of \(X\). \(\square\)

This theorem also explains why passing to a wreath product does not automatically evade the alternating obstruction. On a nontrivial factor cycle, the prime-order condition makes the base labels a removable cocycle. On fixed factors, all surviving order-\(p\) and order-\(q\) labels can still be moved into the same \(d\)-set stabilizer.

## 4. A proved linear subcase for projective special linear chief factors

Put
\[
R=\max\{p,q\},\qquad L=\operatorname{lcm}(1,2,\ldots,R),\qquad B=RL.
\]

### Lemma 4.1 (bounded-order projective transformations)

Let \(n\ge B\), let \(F\) be a finite field, and let \(r\in\{p,q\}\). Every element of order dividing \(r\) in \(\operatorname{PGL}_n(F)\) stabilizes an \(L\)-dimensional subspace of the natural module.

### Proof

Represent the projective element by \(A\in\operatorname{GL}_n(F)\). Then \(A^r=\lambda I\) for some \(\lambda\in F^*\). If \(\operatorname{char}F\ne r\), the polynomial \(T^r-\lambda\) is separable and every irreducible rational block has dimension at most \(r\). If \(\operatorname{char}F=r\), the field is perfect and \(T^r-\lambda=(T-\mu)^r\), so every Jordan block again has dimension at most \(r\). Thus the module is a direct sum of invariant blocks whose dimensions belong to \(\{1,\ldots,R\}\).

If no block size \(e\) occurred at least \(L/e\) times, then
\[
n\le\sum_{e=1}^{R}e(L/e-1)=RL-\frac{R(R+1)}2<B,
\]
a contradiction. Hence some size \(e\) occurs at least \(L/e\) times, and the sum of \(L/e\) such blocks is an invariant \(L\)-subspace. \(\square\)

### Theorem 4.2 (inner-diagonal \(\operatorname{PSL}\) chief-factor obstruction)

Let \(S=\operatorname{PSL}_n(F)\) with \(n\ge B\). Suppose a quotient \(X\) satisfies
\[
S^t\le X\le \operatorname{PGL}_n(F)^t\rtimes S_t.
\]
Then \(X\) cannot be invariably generated by elements of orders \(p\) and \(q\).

### Proof

Let \(P_L\le\operatorname{PGL}_n(F)\) stabilize a fixed \(L\)-subspace. Lemma 4.1 and transitivity of \(\operatorname{PSL}_n(F)\) on \(L\)-subspaces show that every fixed-factor component of order \(p\) or \(q\) is \(S\)-conjugate into \(P_L\). On nontrivial factor cycles, the same coboundary argument as in Theorem 3.1 trivializes the edge labels using \(\operatorname{PGL}_n(F)^t\). The conjugators may be adjusted into \(S^t\) without leaving \(P_L\wr S_t\), because \(P_L\) maps surjectively onto \(\operatorname{PGL}_n(F)/\operatorname{PSL}_n(F)\): diagonal matrices stabilizing the chosen subspace realize every determinant class. Thus the two generators have independent \(S^t\)-conjugates in the common proper subgroup \(X\cap(P_L\wr S_t)\), a contradiction. \(\square\)

The theorem deliberately excludes field and graph automorphisms. Treating them as if they were ordinary projective linear transformations would be an unjustified hidden assumption.

## 5. Assembly toward a counterexample to the root statement

Let \(A_m\le H\), and take a chief series of \(H\). Since \(A_m\) is simple, at the first chief-series term whose intersection with \(A_m\) is nontrivial, the intersection jumps from \(1\) to \(A_m\). Hence \(A_m\) embeds in a chief factor \(S^t\). At least one coordinate projection \(A_m\to S\) is nontrivial and therefore injective. Thus every host of \(A_m\) has a nonabelian simple chief constituent containing \(A_m\).

Theorem 3.1 rules out all sufficiently large alternating constituents of a \((p,q)\)-invariably generated host. Theorem 4.2 rules out high-dimensional \(\operatorname{PSL}\) constituents whenever the chief action has no field or graph part. Sporadic constituents form a finite list. Exceptional Lie-type constituents have bounded defining representation dimension, so standard lower bounds for faithful modular projective representations of \(A_m\) would bound \(m\) in those families.

Therefore the shortest prospective negative proof is:

1. Prove a uniform common-overgroup theorem for order-\(p\) and order-\(q\) automorphisms of every sufficiently high-rank finite classical simple group, including field and graph automorphisms and arbitrary multiplicity \(S^t\).
2. Use the same wreath-gauge argument to exclude those groups as chief constituents.
3. Apply the classification of finite simple groups and a characteristic-uniform lower bound for the projective representation degree of \(A_m\) to obtain a constant \(M(p,q)\) such that no simple chief constituent of a \((p,q)\)-invariably generated group contains \(A_m\) for \(m>M(p,q)\).
4. Then \(A_m\), for any \(m>M(p,q)\), is a genuine counterexample to the root statement.

The unresolved step is theorem-level and sharply localized: it is not enough merely to know that each bounded-order classical automorphism has some invariant subspace. One needs a single proper subgroup type, compatible with the relevant outer-automorphism cosets, meeting every order-\(p\) and order-\(q\) class so that the coordinate gauge adjustment remains inside the socle.

## 6. Sanity checks and failure autopsy

- For \(p=2,q=3,n=7\), every involution and every element of order \(3\) in \(S_7\) stabilizes a six-set, so independently chosen conjugates lie in a common point stabilizer. This is the smallest strict-bound illustration.
- At \(n=pq\), the common \(pq\)-set may be the whole set; no conclusion is claimed there.
- For \(p=2,q=3\), Lemma 4.1 gives \(R=3,L=6,B=18\): every projective involution or projective element of order three in dimension at least eighteen has an invariant six-space.
- The naive positive construction using a regular wreath product with a nonabelian base fails because prime-order lifts on nontrivial top cycles are coboundaries and can be independently conjugated to erase their base data. Fixed-factor labels remain, but for alternating base factors all such labels lie, after independent conjugation, in the same \(pq\)-set stabilizer.
- No conclusion is claimed yet for symplectic, unitary, or orthogonal factors, nor for field/graph-twisted \(\operatorname{PSL}\) actions.

## 7. Source adaptation notes

1. R. M. Guralnick, J. Shareshian, and R. Woodroofe, *On invariable generation of alternating groups by elements of prime and prime power order*, arXiv:2201.12371. Section 2 states the maximal-subgroup criterion used to organize alternating-group invariable generation; Lemma 3.4 states that a base-\(p\) element fixes an \(i\)-set exactly when \(p\nmid\binom ni\). Their positive results choose a prime \(r\) growing with \(n\), often \(r>\sqrt n\). That varying-prime hypothesis is precisely what the present fixed-\(p,q\) obstruction lacks: when both primes are fixed and \(n>pq\), every relevant element has a common possible invariant-set size \(pq\). The paper does not prove the chief-factor theorem above; that deduction is local and self-contained here.
2. C. G. Cox, *Invariable generation and wreath products*, arXiv:2001.04748, Lemma 2.1: in a permutational wreath product, conjugation moves a base coordinate along the head action, while a conjugate of a head element decomposes into a head conjugate times a base element. Cox's finite-generation results use larger invariable generating sets and do not preserve a prescribed two-element order pair. The local cycle-product/gauge argument above explains the additional obstruction for the current problem.
3. D. Bubboloni, P. Spiga, and T. Weigel, *Normal 2-coverings of the finite simple groups and their generalizations*, arXiv:2208.08756, supplies the normal-covering language relating proper subgroup conjugates to invariable generation. It does not state the fixed-order high-rank common-overgroup theorem required here.

## 8. Self-check

- Quotients inherit invariable generation, and the centralizer quotient used in Theorem 3.1 contains the centerless chief factor faithfully.
- \(\operatorname{Aut}(A_n)=S_n\) is used only for \(n\ge7\).
- \(A_n\) is transitive on proper nonempty \(d\)-subsets, and \(K=S_d\times S_{n-d}\) contains both parity classes, which is needed to replace \(S_n^t\)-gauge transformations by \(A_n^t\)-transformations.
- The projective-linear block bound treats defining and nondefining characteristic separately.
- Theorem 4.2 assumes all induced factor automorphisms are inner-diagonal; field and graph automorphisms remain an explicit debt.
- The dossier proves substantial obstruction lemmas but does not claim that the root question has yet been settled.
