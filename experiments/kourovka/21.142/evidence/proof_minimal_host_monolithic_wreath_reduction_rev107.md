# Minimal-host monolithic reduction

## Proposition
Fix distinct primes p and q and an integer m at least 5. Suppose A is a subgroup of a finite group G, A is isomorphic to A_m, and G is invariably generated by x and y with |x|=p and |y|=q. Choose such a quadruple (G,A,x,y) with |G| minimal, allowing the embedded copy of A_m to vary.

Then:

1. Every nontrivial normal subgroup of G contains A.
2. G has a unique minimal normal subgroup N. It is nonabelian and has the form N=S^t for a nonabelian finite simple group S, and A is contained in N.
3. C_G(N)=1, so conjugation identifies G with a subgroup satisfying S^t <= G <= Aut(S) wr Sym(t).
4. At least one coordinate projection N=S^t -> S restricts injectively to A. In particular A_m embeds in S.

Consequently, to prove that A_m cannot embed in any finite group invariably generated by elements of orders p and q, it is enough to prove that for every nonabelian finite simple group S containing A_m, every t at least 1, and every X with S^t <= X <= Aut(S) wr Sym(t), no elements of orders p and q invariably generate X.

## Proof
Let 1 != K be normal in G. Assume first that A intersect K is trivial. Put Q=G/K and write bars for images in Q. The restriction A -> Q is injective, so Q contains a nonabelian subgroup isomorphic to A_m.

Invariable generation passes to Q: for arbitrary u,v in Q choose lifts g,h in G. Then

Q = <(bar x)^u,(bar y)^v>,

because this equality is the image of G=<x^g,y^h>. Since p and q are prime, the orders of bar x and bar y belong respectively to {1,p} and {1,q}. Neither image can be trivial. Indeed, if bar x=1, then taking any v gives Q=<(bar y)^v>, so Q is cyclic; this contradicts the presence of the nonabelian subgroup bar A. The same argument excludes bar y=1. Thus bar x and bar y have exact orders p and q, Q is invariably generated by them, and Q contains a copy of A_m. Since K is nontrivial, |Q|<|G|, contrary to the defining minimality of G. Hence A intersect K is nontrivial.

Because K is normal in G, A intersect K is normal in A. The group A is nonabelian simple for m at least 5, so A intersect K=A. Therefore A<=K for every nontrivial normal subgroup K of G.

A finite group has a minimal nontrivial normal subgroup. Let N be one. The preceding paragraph gives A<=N, so N is nonabelian. If N_1 and N_2 were distinct minimal normal subgroups of G, then N_1 intersect N_2 would be normal in G and proper in each, hence trivial. But both N_i contain A, so their intersection contains A, a contradiction. Thus N is unique.

The subgroup N is characteristically simple: a nontrivial proper characteristic subgroup of N would be a nontrivial proper normal subgroup of G contained in N. A finite characteristically simple group is a direct product of isomorphic simple groups. For completeness, choose a minimal normal subgroup S_1 of the abstract group N and take its orbit under Aut(N). Distinct orbit members intersect trivially and commute, while their product is nontrivial and characteristic in N; hence that product is all of N. Thus N=S_1 x ... x S_t with all S_i isomorphic to a simple group S. Since N contains the nonabelian group A, S is nonabelian.

Now let C=C_G(N). Since N is normal, C is normal in G. If C were nontrivial, the universal containment property already proved would give A<=C. It also gives A<=N. But

C intersect N = Z(N)=1,

because N is a direct product of centerless nonabelian simple groups. This contradiction proves C_G(N)=1.

Conjugation therefore embeds G faithfully into Aut(N), and the image contains Inn(N). Every automorphism of N permutes its simple direct factors, and after this permutation its restrictions are automorphisms of S. Hence

Aut(N) is isomorphic to Aut(S)^t semidirect Sym(t) = Aut(S) wr Sym(t).

Under this identification Inn(N)=Inn(S)^t is identified with S^t. Thus S^t<=G<=Aut(S) wr Sym(t).

Finally, write pi_i:N->S for the coordinate projections. Since A is nontrivial, some pi_i has nontrivial restriction to A. Its kernel on A is normal in the simple group A, and it is not all of A; therefore the kernel is trivial. Hence A embeds in S.

For the consequence, suppose an arbitrary p,q-invariably generated host containing A_m existed and choose one of minimal order. The proposition places it in one of the stated wreath intervals with a simple factor S containing A_m. If every such interval admits independent S^t-conjugates of its order-p and order-q elements inside a common proper subgroup, those elements cannot invariably generate the host, a contradiction.

## Interface with the integrated classical modules
The verified PSL, PSp, PSU, and orthogonal outer-coset common-overgroup theorems apply exactly to the interval produced above whenever S is a sufficiently high-dimensional classical simple group. Therefore the arbitrary-host problem has been reduced without loss to the terminal analysis of simple factors S containing A_m: large alternating factors and the classical bounded-dimension, exceptional, and sporadic families not covered by those modules.

## Self-check
- The quotient argument uses K nontrivial for strict order reduction and A intersect K trivial for the injected copy of A_m.
- The possibility that one generator dies in the quotient is handled explicitly: the quotient would then be cyclic, not merely one-generated in some weaker sense.
- No normality of A in G is assumed; only A intersect K normal in A is used.
- Simplicity requires m at least 5, which is sufficient for the eventual choice of a large alternating counterexample candidate.
- A may be diagonally embedded in S^t. The conclusion is only that one coordinate projection is injective, which is exactly what is needed to obtain A_m<=S.
- Minimality is essential: without it, an ambient direct product can have a nontrivial normal subgroup disjoint from the displayed copy of A.
- No external classification or citation is used in this lemma.
