Concrete lower-bound stress test. Let G=SL(2,3), let Z=Z(G)={±I}, and use G/Z≅A4. For a subgroup H≅C3 of A4, set M to be its full inverse image in G. The subgroup H is maximal in A4, so M is maximal in G; moreover M≅C6 and hence d(M)=1. Writing G≅Q8⋊C3, the element of order 3 cyclically permutes the three noncentral pairs of elements of Q8, so [Q8,C3]=Q8. Therefore G'=Q8, G''=Q8'=Z, and G'''=1, giving d(G)=3. Thus d(G)-d(M)=2. The full hypotheses are satisfied: G is finite and soluble and M is maximal. Consequently every universal constant in the root problem must satisfy k≥2. This does not refute existence of a universal constant. It does refute the structural conjecture that passage from (G,M) to the primitive core quotient controls the entire gap: here d(G/Z)-d(M/Z)=d(A4)-d(C3)=1, whereas the original gap is 2. The competing structural conjectures were (C1) a core extension cannot increase the primitive-quotient gap, which would direct the proof toward a bound k≤1, and (C2) a central core can contribute an unmatched derived layer. The example decides for C2. The remaining competing root-level possibilities are that this is the unique extra layer and the optimum is k=2, or that iterated core extensions produce gaps at least 3, possibly unbounded. Verdict on the full root question: not_refuted; smallest unsupported interface is control of further unmatched derived layers inside core_G(M).
