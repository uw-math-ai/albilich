# POONEN-SPK — Bjorn Poonen, "Practical Suggestions for Mathematical Speaking" (fetched 2026-07-08, PDF read in full, Mar 28 2022; exposition-relevant rules extracted)

Writing-relevant rules (talk-logistics items 11-12, 16-18, 21-31, 35 omitted as speech-only):
1. (1) What is written should stand alone as a coherent exposition.
2. (3) Write the little words — "so", "then", "therefore", "thus", "because", "on the other hand" — to make the logic clear.
3. (4) Make conditional statements explicit: "If …, then …".
4. (5) Always make quantification explicit. Bad: "a is in the center iff ax = xa." Good: "… iff ax = xa for all x ∈ G."
5. (6) In a theorem statement, write "Then" to separate hypotheses from conclusions.
6. (7) Distinguish statements from definitions: "X = Y" asserts equality of previously defined objects; "Let X = Y" / "X := Y" define. Mark definitions (Def., :=, underline/italicize the defined term).
7. (8) Priority of content: definitions first (without them you don't know what you're talking about), then theorems and examples illustrating them, proofs last — if something must be skipped, skip (easy) proofs.
8. (9) If you skip steps, say so.
9. (10) Write and say only true things; if simplifying to convey an idea, tell the audience.
10. (13/14) Tell the audience up front what they will learn; at the end remind them of the main point/takeaway.
11. (19) A presentation of a paper is an advertisement for the paper (selection, not completeness).
12. (20) A worked special case can be more illuminating than a complete general proof — then present the special case.
13. (32) Credit others' theorems: "\begin{theorem}[Robinson 1949]" — attribution at the statement site.
14. (33-nitpick) Do not use ⇒ to mean "so"/"thus"/"then"; reserve it for talking about the logical implication itself.
15. (30) When less context is visible (slides ≈ short attention contexts), remind the audience of definitions and the current goal as you go.
