# PAK-CLEAR — Igor Pak, "How to Write a Clear Math Paper: Some 21st Century Tips" (fetched 2026-07-08, PDF read in full, 18pp, J. Hum. Math. 8 (2018) 301-328)

## §1 Be clear! (the golden rule)
1. Clarity is not just phrasing. Worked pair: "Abelian groups have trivial center." vs the Galois/Jordan/Struik monstrosity — with the footnote: **the clear statement is mathematically FALSE, and "when you are unclear, all claims look reasonably true."** (Unclear writing masks errors — key rubric insight: clarity is a correctness-verification enabler.)
2. The h(h) test of commitment: if h is a variable in some sections and a function in others, spend the 30 minutes renaming every instance — a per-section disclaimer is NOT acceptable.
3. Why: clarity is not about you. A confused reader on page 3 gives up (grad student uses an older weaker paper because it's better written); a skimming postdoc dismisses your paper as irrelevant (you lose the citation and the area loses a theorem). Clear writing makes junior people be taken seriously and is a competitive advantage when near-identical results appear ("recordings of the same symphony by different orchestras have very different values").
4. Journals won't fix clarity (copy editors catch linguistics, not math); the arXiv version is what people read — the author alone owns clarity.
5. **For the sake of clarity, ignore all rules** (Wikipedia IAR): when style/grammar rules make math unclear, reword first; if nothing works, break the rule, however fundamental.

## §2 Where to start
6. Reading order: native English speakers read Zinsser (On Writing Well) first; non-native speakers read Halmos and short guides first, Zinsser later. Zinsser on paragraphs (quoted approvingly): "Keep your paragraphs short … but don't go berserk. A succession of tiny paragraphs is as annoying as a paragraph that's too long."
7. Modern reading reality: readers skim titles, some read only abstracts, some only introductions, some jump straight to main results, some only hear of work at seminars. **Write to serve ALL these reading styles simultaneously** (Matryoshka-doll structure: title → abstract → intro → main → final remarks).

## §3 Macro tips (structure)
8. **Title**: super important; not too long/short/vague-generic ("On some problems in group theory" = bad); first approximation to content. Emphasize content, not length: proved all → "All tennis balls are white"; some → "On white tennis balls"/"New examples of…"; new short proof → "Short proof that…"; counterexample → "Not all tennis balls are white"; further properties → "A remark on…"; survey → say "survey" in the title ("A short survey" means short in CONTENT — brief, incomplete — not length).
9. Naming trick: name your objects (Munro permutations) and put the name in the title; drawbacks — attribution drift and referee eyebrows.
10. **Abstract**: dry facts, nothing personal; key results first, brief mention of others; no details, no connections to other work unless absolutely necessary; clear, precise, plain, uninventive (many MathSciNet reviews are just the abstract). Rule of thumb: abstract lines ≈ 0.3-0.5 × page count (10 lines for 10 pages is excessive). Citing [Pak12] in the abstract is fine despite journal guidelines — more precise than "in 2012."
11. Table of contents: skip unless > 60 pages.
12. **Introduction**: the hardest section; the only part most readers read. Write its first draft FIRST (so you know what's in the paper), completely rewrite it after the paper is done, let it stew, show colleagues, rewrite again. Rota's lengthy-introduction advice is half-right: write the lengthy Rota-style intro, keep a few pages, move the rest to Final Remarks.
13. Introduction content: set up problem + state main results; include the few technical definitions needed; state the conjecture being resolved (with attribution); **first theorem on page 1, at worst page 2.** Result-free introductions work only under 1.5 pages in a ≤10-page note. If the main theorem is too technical to state: state interesting, nontrivial, easy-to-state COROLLARIES instead — "cook them up if necessary"; the corollary is what passers-by remember and retell.
14. Introduction exclusions: technical definitions, examples, big figures — replace with forward links "(see Figure 5.1)", "(see the exact definition in §3.4)".
15. Anti-Rota correction: don't "give everybody his due" in the intro — impossible to control length; explain only the history DIRECTLY relevant to the main result (worked example: "[A] asked XYZ and proved weak-XYZ; [B] showed strong-XYZ false; we refine [B]'s tools to show XYZ false; we conjecture weak-XYZ is best possible"). Full history goes to Final Remarks.
16. Last paragraph of intro: outline the structure of the paper in words (readers navigate with it).
17. **Foreword** (for intros > 4 pages): first subsection of the Introduction, a nontechnical introduction-to-the-Introduction; big picture, motivational examples in other fields, physical/philosophical principles; keep under a page. Even in short papers, write the FIRST PARAGRAPH in this style: "Nothing is less inspiring than a paper which starts 'Let G = (V,E) be a loopless graph on n vertices.'" Math writing is rigid, so the opening "is your only place to shine. Use it!"
18. **Final Remarks = endnotes** (the least understood section): untitled subsections (\subsection{}), each 1-2 paragraphs to a page, ordered by decreasing importance, mutually independent (not meant to be read in order). First subsection: expanded history "giving everybody his due." Then: future directions, own speculative conjectures, others' conjectures. While writing the main part, drop placeholder subsections here and insert "(for more on this, see §6.1)" links in the text. Size objections: extract big subsections into "Open problems"/"Historical overview" sections; or cut for the journal but keep the full arXiv version; or set the section in \small — all fine. Bonus: dynamically updateable arXiv semi-survey (add subsections without touching the rest; stake priority before the next paper).
19. **Acknowledgements**: short unnumbered section before References, smaller font OK. Order of increasing importance: (a) everyone you discussed the results with, by name, alphabetical ("helpful conversations"; nicknames as used in their email sign-offs are fine); (b) singled-out people with the specific contribution spelled out ("his invisible hand Lemma … leading to a much shorter proof of Theorem 3.4"), one by one; (c) hosting institutions, then granting agencies. Don't email for permission to thank — EXCEPT when using private information (e.g., a conjecture told to you); if asked to remove it, do so.

## §4 References (Pak's signature contribution — citation semantics)
20. Why references matter: "They are of utmost importance — they are what makes the whole system work." Everyone notices where and how they are cited; careless citing creates decades-long grudges.
21. **The 12-level citation-semantics ladder** (decreasing importance/reliability; each phrasing MEANS something different):
    (1) "Roth proved Murakami's conjecture in [Roth]." — clear, definitive.
    (2) "… conjecture [Roth]." — proved it, possibly elsewhere; likely definitive version.
    (3) "… conjecture, see [Roth]." — [Roth] could be original, follow-up, or survey; "see [Melville]" may mean Roth never wrote it up and Melville published it.
    (4) "… [Roth], see also [Woolf]." — Woolf made an important contribution (generality, major gap fixes).
    (5) "… in [Roth] (see also [Woolf])." — Woolf has a complete proof, fixing minor errors.
    (6) "… (see [Woolf])." — [Woolf] (e.g. the standard monograph) is the definitive version.
    (7) "… see e.g. [F1,F2,F3]." — validity confirmed in several books/surveys; or the author was too lazy to find the right reference.
    (8) "… (see e.g. [F1,F2,F3])." — classical/very well known; nobody will check.
    (9) footnote "See [Mailer]." — author likely never read [Mailer]; can't verify; visibly annoyed.
    (10) footnote "Love letter from H. Fielding to J. Austen, dated…" — document exists with proof/outline; googling will surface the story.
    (11) footnote "Personal communication." — claimed over email/beer; proof may or may not be correct.
    (12) "Roth claims to have proved … in [Roth]." — well-known unfixed gap, or erroneous-but-famous (often followed by "(see, however, [Atwood])"), or a 3-page Doklady outline nobody completed.
22. **Never cite a range**: "See [2–19] for some relevant work" is "a disservice to the community" — helps no reader, gives no author credit, pits authors against each other. Go paper by paper, most important first, describing each contribution; stop when tired. (Worked partitions-into-even-primes example, including "the paper remains under scrutiny (my colleague Kiran X. has been unsuccessful in independently verifying the result)".)
23. **Where to cite**: most relevant papers in the Introduction; the rest in Final Remarks; NOTHING in the main part — "there is no way to be fair, clear and complete when citing in the middle of the paper without breaking the flow." Major exception: using somebody's result as a lemma → precise reference at the point of use, no elaboration (its story goes in a Remark after the proof or in Final Remarks).
24. **Pin-cite stability**: when quoting a specific result from anything > 5 pages, give its location; prefer arXiv-version section/subsection numbers ("see [A, §3.1]") — page numbers and theorem numbers shift across versions. (Explicitly contradicts Elsevier guidelines; Pak says do it anyway.)
25. Reference style: no BibTeX unless expert (MathSciNet entries bloated, inconsistent); pick one style, stick to it; concise but findable (abbreviate first names, omit series titles/issue numbers; physics-style title omission goes too far). Alphanumeric labels for longer papers: [SY09, Woo96]; unpublished → [Con17+]; ≥5 authors → [A+13]; same author-year → [Tra15a,b]. Don't emulate Knuth's perfectionism ("Richard Peter Stanley" confuses); no Cyrillic titles/Chinese characters. Unpublished papers: include arXiv number or link; hyperref + url; shorten links.

## §5 Micro tips
26. **Don't be pedantic**: ignore Serre's objection to ℚ ⊂ ℝ; for standard/easy notions extra explanations distract ("unless you are a member of Bourbaki"). Domino tilings: don't define region-covering formalities — "Just draw a picture and get on with your math."
27. **Downshift your style** (anti-Berndt): the audience includes readers with limited English, short attention spans, no patience. "It is perfectly ok to be repetitive and have ten therefore's … rather than vary the style and have difficult words like 'henceforth'." Remind the reader of earlier notation. (Berndt's response footnoted: captivating style still matters — both recorded.)
28. Tense: outside Introduction/Final Remarks use present indefinite, occasionally past indefinite. Write short sentences.
29. Commas exist to make sentence STRUCTURE clear, not to mark speech pauses: put commas on both sides of every clause/subclause ("Consider an integer x, whose square is 4, and which is positive."). Ignore the Oxford-comma debate; ignore Strunk & White punctuation (use Huddleston-Pullum if a guide is needed).
30. **No semicolons** — "it implies a logical connection between the clauses, which is best made explicit between the sentences."
31. Long dashes: leave spaces at both ends.
32. Spelling: most common form, Standard American English (no "coöperation", no "colouring"); close calls (dominoes/dominos) → let Google Search break the tie.
33. **No morphological variations of defined terms**: if you define "nice graph", never write "niceness of graphs implies…" — stick to the basic form.
34. LaTeX macros for everything (\al, \rT, \lra), same macros across all papers; global notation changes become one-line edits; comment out a macro to check for symbol collisions.
35. Letter choice: Ξ, ι, ϖ, ı, ȷ, κ look weird; κ, υ too close to k, v; ∅ not \emptyset (computer-zero lookalike); **always ℓ not l; always ε not ϵ**; ϕ and φ sufficiently different to coexist. Mnemonics: tailed letters = functions (f,g,ϕ,φ,ζ,ξ,η,ρ); a-e constants; x,y,z variables; i,j,k,ℓ,m,n integers. Placeholder macros + font play at the end to keep similar-looking letters apart; other alphabets fine (ℶ need not be a cardinal); avoid Gothic fonts (G vs S, F vs T). Local emphasis tags: (∗), (⋄), (♥) fine.
36. **Do NOT trust LaTeX spacing**: Φ(2a+c)Φ(c−2a)… reads as ⊕-like separators; insert \, between factors. Partition 12+10+10+2+1 in short form "1210221" is unparseable → "12 10 2 2 1".
37. Figures: many but SMALL (3-5 cm); big detailed figures break flow — make several aligned copies with increasing detail levels; half-page figures → appendix or website with a link.

## §6 When you are done
38. Never really done: stew a week; reread fresh for typos/bad wording/imprecise statements; post on website + email colleagues BEFORE arXiv; attend to comments, then arXiv; keep updating website/arXiv after publication. **But: a wrong proof of a lemma or a missing key reference (rediscovery) cannot be whitewashed in an update — it warrants a separate "Erratum" or "Acknowledgement of attribution" file.**
39. Advertise: link recorded talks + slides next to the paper; blog the gist. Harder: write a survey; extract an olympiad problem from the simplest nontrivial special case (send to olympiad/Putnam committees); or an undergraduate-accessible note for Monthly/Intelligencer/Math Magazine explaining at the end how the special case fits the paper.
40. Rewrite-and-republish compromise (vs Rota's several-times advice): if a published paper is ignored because unclear, rewrite, simplify, mildly generalize, add examples, re-post (at least on arXiv). OK to start with a proof of a known theorem "for completeness" then explain the modification for generality — "a clear presentation of your own old result and a clear pathway to your new generalization."
