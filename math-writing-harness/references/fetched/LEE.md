# LEE — Kevin P. Lee, "A Guide to Writing Mathematics" (fetched 2026-07-08, PDF read in full, 17pp)

Framing: "The ideas are the mathematics. So a page of computations without any writing or explanation contains no math." Writing math ≠ "showing your work."

## Combining words and equations
1. Good writing observes grammar; math is written in sentences and paragraphs (a three-page single paragraph is not amusing). No irregular abbreviations.
2. Formulas follow grammatical rules: "=" acts like a verb ("1+1=2." is a complete sentence; so are "3xy < 2.", "5z ∈ R.", "∃s ≠ t."); an expression without a relation is a noun, not a sentence.
3. Formulas and equations are contained in complete sentences with punctuation: period if the computation ends the sentence, comma otherwise.
4. Read your writing INCLUDING the equations out loud — ears catch fragments better than eyes.
5. "We" (first-person plural) is standard and welcome.
6. Important or long formulas go on display lines of their own (worked Bad/Good "Flugelputz-Levitator" example).
7. Do not misuse "=": never use it for "the next step is"/"implies" (a chained mis-equality literally asserts −1 = 0 = 1). Arrows instead of equals are only a slight improvement. For a multi-step computation, put each equation on its own line; for hard steps, add words describing each step.
8. No "two-column" proof/computation format (equation | reason columns) — hard to read, bad style; the paper must have a single flow.
9. Words vs symbols each where they serve best: "It follows that x plus two is larger than zero" → "It follows that x + 2 > 0"; conversely don't symbolize what reads better as words.
10. Don't start a sentence with a formula: "Since t = 5 when w = 2000, …"; "The function f is globberfluxible at x = 3."
11. Use language precisely; avoid vague words and overuse of "it"/"that" — if the author can't say what "it" refers to, the ideas need more thought.
12. Write simply and directly; no ponderous pretentious prose.

## Organizing
13. Introduction states the problem in your own words (reader has no textbook), discusses significance ("hook"), and previews the paper.
14. "Answer" may go right after the intro (result-first) or at the end (method-first) — a deliberate choice.
15. State the result in terms of the original problem in real-world terms ("The population of Utopia is at its smallest 6 years after the plague begins," not just "t = 6").
16. Outline before writing; organization matters more as problems get harder.

## Audience & persuasion
17. Assume a reader with your background who doesn't know this problem (default: a student in another section). The purpose is not to show the professor.
18. A good paper persuades a skeptical reader: alternate routes to the same solution, pictures/graphs, specific examples, extreme cases showing limits of an argument.
19. Relevance: extraneous comments demonstrate lack of understanding; omit tedious calculations; keep everything critical to the key ideas.
20. State ALL assumptions, physical and mathematical (frictionless? continuous? linear? real?) — the reader won't assume them for you.

## Defining variables and formulas
21. State what every letter represents, precisely: "For any whole number n, either n or n+1 is even" (not a bare "Either n or n+1 is even" — what is n?).
22. "Let …" introduces variables; prefer binding function definitions fully: "Let f(x) = x² + 1 for all real numbers x."
23. If describing all variables gets tedious, remove variables: "The volume of the box is the product of the length, the width, and the height."
24. Real-world variables get units and specificity: "Let D(t) be Agnes's distance from the arena in miles t hours after the riot began."
25. Each symbol represents ONE thing. (Worked wombat example: P silently shifts from population-function to two particular values; fix by substituting values and naming t₂₀₀₀.) "A symbol used in two different ways is not only confusing, but often results in incorrect mathematics!"
26. Don't pull formulas out of thin air — say where each comes from or what it means.

## Pictures
27. Visual arguments encouraged, but fully explain how each picture fits the argument ("Looking at the graph, we can see the result is true" → say WHAT to look at and WHY it supports the claim).
28. Graphs neatly drawn (straight edge or software), axes labeled with units, scale indicated, significant features pointed out; label everything referenced.

## Final checklist (verbatim headings)
Neatly typed? Proofread? Introduction (problem, why interesting, preview)? All assumptions stated? Grammar/spelling/punctuation correct, complete sentences incl. equations? All variables defined precisely with units? Symbols used correctly (no "=" misuse)? Words precise (no vague pronouns)? Diagrams/graphs labeled, scaled? Mathematics correct? Did you SOLVE the problem and state it in real-world terms?
