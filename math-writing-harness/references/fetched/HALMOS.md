# HALMOS — P.R. Halmos, "How to Write Mathematics" (fetched 2026-07-08, full 22pp PDF read; the canonical essay, L'Enseignement Math. 16 (1970))

The foundational essay. Section-by-section rules, with verbatim examples where the essay gives them.

## §1-2 There is no recipe / Say something
1. The complete recipe: have something to say, have someone to say it to, organize it, arrange it, write it, rewrite it, re-rewrite it, and work hard on mechanical details (diction, notation, punctuation).
2. Two ways to lack a subject: no ideas, or too many ("calculus books are bad because there is no such subject as calculus; it is not a subject because it is many subjects"). A unit of writing must have ONE delineated subject that "hangs together."
3. Having something to say is the most important ingredient — important ideas survive bad exposition (Birkhoff's ergodic proof), but that's rare and never desirable.

## §3 Speak to someone
4. Specify the audience concretely — ideally one specific person ("perhaps someone I discussed the subject with two years ago"). Keeping a specific reader in mind is ESSENTIAL for anticipating and forestalling the reader's difficulties.
5. Avoid the temptation of a sharply-specified audience: snide polemic comments and heavy-handed "in" jokes.
6. "The writer must anticipate and avoid the reader's difficulties … keep trying to imagine what in the words being written may tend to mislead the reader, and what will set him right."
7. Better a definite aim that misses than a vague inclusive one: "Get ready, aim, and fire."

## §4 Organize first
8. The expositor's main contribution IS organization: "minimize the resistance and maximize the insight of the reader and keep him on the track with no unintended distractions."
9. Ideal outline: every heuristic discussion, lemma, theorem, corollary, remark, proof placed in an order both logically correct and psychologically digestible. Reader told early what to expect, yet pleasant surprises still happen.
10. Organization (tree of headings + connections) matters more than arrangement (linearization), but both matter.
11. Tight organization test (the botched-lectures anecdote): things are there because they are needed; wires invisible until tampered with.
12. What to leave out matters as much as what to put in: "too much detail can be as discouraging as none" (anti-Bourbaki). "The heart of mathematics consists of concrete examples and concrete problems" — organize around the central, crucial examples and counterexamples.
13. Guide the reader on what the proof does NOT prove: appropriate counterexamples, where to go next.

## §5-6 Think about the alphabet (notation design)
14. Design notation at the beginning, not ad hoc mid-sentence ("ad hoc decisions about notation, made mid-sentence in the heat of composition, are almost certain to result in bad notation").
15. Alphabetical harmony: ax + by or a₁x₁ + a₂x₂, NOT ax₁ + bx₂. Avoid Σ as index set forcing ∑_{σ∈Σ} a_σ. Don't use |z| < ε at top of page and z ε U at bottom (∈ reserved for membership, ε for ad hoc use). Near-dissonance causes "a vague non-localized feeling of malaise."
16. Do not freeze letters ("xyz-space"; matrices with "property L" — frozen and unsuggestive). Respect existing freezes: e, i, π, 0-9; don't use n for a complex number, ε for a positive integer, z for a topological space ("A mathematician's nightmare is a sequence nε that tends to 0 as ε becomes infinite.")
17. Avoid letter-collision designs like Lefschetz's x^p_i chain vs x_p^i cochain ("what is x²₃?").

## §7 Write in spirals
18. Spiral plan: write 1,2,1,2,3,1,2,3,4,…; each new chapter forces rewriting of earlier ones. Applies to chapters, sections, paragraphs, sentences, words.
19. First draft: spill everything, violate all rules, just keep writing. THEN rewrite mercilessly — "Rewrite means write again — every word," not red-pencil edits. (Chapter 1 of a 10-chapter book gets written ~3-4 times.)
20. Steady production: write daily; end each session by priming the next (tomorrow's first sentence, next section's title).
21. Title test for sections: "if you cannot find a title, the reason may be that the section doesn't have a single unified subject."

## §8 Organize always
22. Spiral organization: each new section reviews all previous sections from its new point of view (vector spaces → linear dependence: which of §1's examples are dependent/independent?). "The reader is helped by the firm organizational scaffolding."
23. Subplots and clues (novelist's devices): a word first mentioned chapters before its definition, re-mentioned with increasing detail, is "inconspicuous, subliminal preparation for its full-dress introduction." "If you work eight hours to save five minutes of the reader's time, you have saved over 80 man-hours for each 1000 readers."
24. Logical arrangement constraint: no statement may be used before it is proved (his 1000-sheets-of-paper topological sort anecdote); among the many valid orders pick the most efficient and pleasant.

## §9 Write good English
25. Good English = correct grammar, correct word choice, correct punctuation, common sense — "completely unobtrusive, like good background music for a movie." Not pedantic, heavy-handed, formal, bureaucratic, flowery.
26. Know that/which and less/fewer distinctions; "a hundred pages of colloquial misusage … has a cumulative abrasive effect." A single missing comma won't break a proof, but consistent mistreatment of small things has large effects.
27. Substitute your own language for "English" — the rule is language-correctness, not English per se.

## §10 Honesty is the best policy
28. Style should be "good in the sense of perfect unobtrusiveness"; clarity, not pedantry; understanding, not fuss. But never gloss over difficulties: if the only known proof is a cumbersome computation, carry it out in public, with sympathy ("unfortunately the only known proof is the following cumbersome computation").
29. **Status honesty (the "p does not imply q" rule)**: whenever you assert a non-implication or side remark, tell the reader where it stands — Is it obvious? (Say so.) Counterexample later? (Promise it now.) Standard literature? (Give a reference.) You tried and failed and don't know? (Confess immediately!) "Take the reader into your confidence."
30. Rules for "obvious"/"easy to see": you thought it obvious when you wrote it; still obvious months later; accepted as obvious when explained to a friend/seminar (by demonstration, not intimidation); and above all — make sure the "obvious" is true.
31. "Tell him where it stands: this has been proved, that hasn't, this will be proved, that won't. Emphasize the important and minimize the trivial."
32. "Pretentiousness, bluff, and concealment may not get caught out immediately, but most readers will soon sense that there is something wrong, and they will blame … the author. Complete honesty makes for greatest clarity."

## §11 Down with the irrelevant and the trivial
33. No irrelevant assumptions: "If R is a commutative semisimple ring with unit and if x and y are in R, then x²−y² = (x−y)(x+y)" — the alert reader wastes time wondering what semisimplicity has to do with it.
34. Mention and exclude trivial cases explicitly (every complex number = non-negative × modulus-1: say what's fishy about 0). Legalistically-correct-but-unexplicit is "misleading, bad exposition, bad psychology."
35. **State the theorem first** — before its proof, after motivation. No rambling then "Thus we have proved that…"; no "hanging theorem" ("Thus we have proved\n Theorem 2…").
36. Theorem statement: ONE sentence when possible (simple implication or declaration); no chit-chat ("Without loss of generality we may assume…" and "Moreover it follows from Theorem 1 that…" do NOT belong in the statement).
37. Page-long theorems with eight hypotheses and six conclusions "do not a theorem make; they are a badly expounded theory" — either hypotheses are removable (bad statement) or they describe a concept that deserves to be isolated, named, and studied.

## §12 Do and do not repeat
38. GOOD repetition: word-for-word repetition of a parallel passage so the one difference stands out — WITH a "roll of drums" ("Note that the first five conditions in the definitions of p and q are the same; what makes them different is the weakening of the sixth.").
39. BAD repetition #1: saying the same thing twice in slightly different words — the reader wonders whether the new statement differs from the old ("even the dimmest such wonder is bad").
40. BAD repetition #2: never repeat a proof. "By the same technique as in the proof of Theorem 1…" or "see the proof of Theorem 1" signal a lemma worth finding, formulating, and proving.

## §13 The editorial we / voice
41. Preferred: neutral, factual, simple declarative sentences ("Since p, it follows that q." "This implies p."). Never "one has thus proved that…" (awful).
42. Imperative is effective: "To find p, multiply q by r." "Given p, put q equal to r."
43. "Given": don't use it when it means nothing ("For any given p there is a q"); don't leave it dangling (not "Given p, there is a q" but "Given p, find q").
44. "We" = "the author and the reader" — fine ("Using Lemma 2 we can generalize Theorem 1"); wrong when it means the author alone ("Our work on this result was done in 1969"; "We thank our wife for her help" is always bad).
45. "I" usually repellent in papers (arrogance/ex-cathedra); acceptable in essays and personal remarks.

## §14 Use words correctly
46. **Never use "any" in mathematical writing** — ambiguous between ∃ ("have you any wool?") and ∀ ("any number can play"). Replace by "each"/"every" or recast. (Worked example: "Prove that any complex number is the product of…" invites the student's "−4i is one such; q.e.d.")
47. Do NOT substitute formal-logic symbolism for prose: ∀z ∃p ∃u[…] "is a cumbersome code … something that machines can write and few but machines can read." (I deny that anybody thinks in terms of ∃, ∀, ∧.)
48. "Where" = "a lazy afterthought that should have been thought through before" ("If n is sufficiently large, then |aₙ| < ε, where ε is a preassigned positive number" — both disease and cure clear).
49. "Equivalent" for theorems is logical nonsense (two true statements are trivially equivalent; what's meant is proof-effort transfer — say that).
50. "If p, then if q, then r" — logically fine, psychologically a pebble to stumble over; recast per local emphasis ("If p and q, then r", etc.).

## §15 Use technical terms correctly
51. Function ≠ its values: not "the function z²+1" but "the function f defined by f(z) = z²+1" or "the function z ↦ z²+1."
52. "Sequence" means indexed by ℕ — "the union of a sequence of measurable sets" misdirects (order irrelevant); say "countable set."
53. Contain (∈) vs include (⊂): Halmos's experiment — consistent use is easy, harmless, unnoticed, and quietly helpful.
54. **"Consistency is a major virtue and its opposite is a cardinal sin in exposition"** — in language, notation, references, typography.
55. Terms: (1) avoid new technical terms whenever possible; (2) think hard about the ones you must create, make them appropriate; (3) use old ones correctly and consistently without obtrusive pedantry.

## §16 Resist symbols
56. "The best notation is no notation" — pretend you're explaining on a long walk in the woods, no paper; use symbols only when really necessary.
57. No irrelevant symbols: "On a compact space every real-valued continuous function f is bounded" — f contributes nothing. "If 0 ≤ limₙ αₙ^{1/n} = ρ < 1, then limₙ αₙ = 0" — ρ is proof-preparation smuggled into the statement; move it into the proof ("Write ρ = …", half a line longer, reads better).
58. "Use no letter only once" (leave no variable free) — convert free variables to bound ones; rule may be bent (a dangling f referred to in the next sentence), not shattered.
59. Never say "g also satisfies (1)" when (1) was displayed about a specific f — "logical and alphabetical nonsense." Say "(1) remains satisfied if f is replaced by g," or better, name the property ("g also belongs to L²(0,1)").
60. **Label displays only when referenced**: an unused label draws attention and wonder that are wasted. (But don't be too stingy — the Dickson "(1)" that appears overleaf cost Halmos "a helpless and bewildered five minutes.")
61. Induction: often "the step from 1 to 2 and an airy 'and so on'" is as rigorous and far more understandable than the full apparatus; a typical 3×3 special case often beats a page of aᵢⱼ's with triple dots.
62. **No unexplained chains of equalities**: a proof that's a chain of expressions separated by = signs is "coding"; the reader must decode as he learns. Write the recipe paragraph instead: "For the proof, first substitute p for q, then collect terms, permute factors, and finally, insert and cancel a factor r."
63. **Write proofs forward**: "Given ε, let δ be (ε/(3M²+2))^{1/2}" is backward proof-writing — machine-verifiable but not human-understandable. Start with something less than ε and do what needs to be done; forward is "graspable and rememberable."

## §17 Use symbols correctly (punctuation & layout)
64. Read each symbol one fixed way: always read ∈ as "is in" (verb). Not "For x ∈ A, we have x ∈ B" (preposition + verb in one sentence); write "For x in A, we have x ∈ B" or "If x ∈ A, then x ∈ B." "Whenever a positive number is ≤ 3, its square is ≤ 9" is ugly.
65. Punctuate symbolic sentences just as verbal ones — an equation/inequality/inclusion is a clause and demands separation from its neighbors.
66. Don't overwork small punctuation marks: "Assume that a ∈ X. X belongs to the class C" — the period between the X's is overworked. **Never start a sentence with a symbol**; use apposition: "The set X belongs to the class C."
67. Overworked comma likewise: not "For invertible X, X* also is invertible" but "For invertible X, the adjoint X* also is invertible"; not "Since p ≠ 0, p ∈ U" but "Since p ≠ 0, it follows that p ∈ U."
68. **Always write "then" with "if"** in mathematical contexts: "The presence of 'then' can never confuse; its absence can."
69. Page architecture: not solid prose (forbidding, sermony), not computational hash (frightening). "Break it up, but not too small; use prose, but not too much. Intersperse enough displays to give the eye a chance to help the brain."

## §18-20 All communication is exposition / Defend your style / Stop
70. Books, papers, lectures, letters differ less than they resemble; content, aim, organization + grammar, diction, notation are the essentials everywhere.
71. Defend deliberate style choices against mechanical rule-application (copyeditor anecdotes: "nonmeasure preserving transformation"; "according as x is positive or negative" reversed for euphony; logical placement of punctuation with quotes).
72. Finishing protocol: read the whole in a gulp testing the stranger's-eye questions — Is the mathematics good, is the exposition interesting, is the language clear, is the format pleasant and easy to read? Proofread, check computations, let the manuscript ripen (months out of sight), re-read rested, then STOP — don't keep polishing ("you'll only discover another mirage just ahead").
73. Naming matters (§20 regrets): "commutator" good, "set of first category" bad; "the closed graph theorem" good, "the Cauchy-Buniakowski-Schwarz theorem" bad — name concepts and theorems descriptively.
74. Follow a model: choose a writer whose writing touches and teaches you; adapt his style to your personality and subject.
