# PAK-STORY — Igor Pak, "How to Tell a Good Mathematical Story" (fetched 2026-07-08, PDF read in full, 6pp, dated Jan 29 2021)

Companion to PAK-CLEAR; this one is about the narrative/framing layer (L3), written for deciding WHAT goes in the paper and around which storyline.

## The story requirement
1. Before "How to write a paper?" comes "What do I write in the paper?" — what to include, what to omit, what to emphasize, in what order. Resolve these by constructing a story about your results.
2. **The story is never personal.** Not "I was standing on my balcony drinking Boba tea when I realized…" — the story is about your AREA, retellable by mathematicians who never heard of you.
3. Lunch test: imagine one senior mathematician telling another "I just saw a paper on the arXiv which does [your story]." The story must be clear enough for a casual reader to pick up and retell without effort.
4. Format: a non-technical, non-detailed version of the abstract — short enough to be a tweet, interesting enough that someone would want to tell it and a listener would ask for details. (Aesop and Terminator worked examples: dry story + enough hints to make the reader curious.)
5. Finding the story: identify the Main Result (the one you want discussed over lunch), then answer — Why is it important? What applications? What is the history? What made it possible for you when nobody else could? The most interesting 1-2 sentence answer to a non-expert IS the story.

## Good story archetypes (verbatim-condensed)
- (1) "We use tools from field pqr to resolve a major open problem X in field abc."
- (2) "We generalize the results in the recent breakthrough paper [M] to work under weaker assumptions."
- (3) "Our recent paper [FGH] provides a theoretical framework; here we work out special cases, rederiving/generalizing earlier work and computing new cases."
- (4) "We point out a crucial mistake in [M]'s proof of X and show X fails in some cases; positively, [M]'s proof can be salvaged in special case X′, so conjecture Y (previously derived from X) survives via X′."
6. Stories need NOT be short: "they have to get longer when the logical structure of the paper is convoluted. Do not aim to make such stories even shorter — that would be a disservice to the reader."

## Weak-story antipatterns
7. (5) "New proof of X, shorter and more elegant" — "elegant" is subjective; the story omits the tool innovation. Must answer: same tools as before? shorter only by omitting easy calculations? can the new proof prove any generalization the old ones couldn't?
8. (6) "We prove a special case already proved in [M]; next paper we hope to prove X" — invites "why not wait and write the next paper?"; speculative stories get dismissed by readers, editors, referees.
9. (7) Straddle-story ("Y′ is of little interest in pqr but translates to abc") — neither audience is served; "since you don't seem to care about abc, and are only using it as an afterthought to justify your paper, why should they care?"
10. A bad story does not mean a bad paper — it means rewrite from a different perspective.

## Truth calibration (critical for a faithfulness rubric)
11. **"On facts, results, mathematical implications, prior work, references, etc., you must be completely, 100% correct. There is no room for flexibility here. Yes, this includes historical discussions."** If you don't know references: search, ask, post on MathOverflow — "definitely don't try to write your guesses and hope for the best."
12. The STORY (framing, motivation, order of discovery) may be constructed post hoc — story (8) worked example: framing the accidental refutation of X as a purposeful narrative is "not how it all happened … mildly misleading, but literally correct." The line: framing is constructible; facts are not. (Thank colleague U profusely in the Acknowledgements.)

## Building the paper around the story
13. Build the Introduction first, then the rest of the paper around the story. Discard everything irrelevant. Gather references in support; when references point the other way (case (4)), "take pains to carefully explain what's going on." History goes partly in the Introduction, partly in Final Remarks.
14. Results that don't fit the story: usually discard (field-dependent). "You don't want to spoil your story and distract the reader from your own main results by cluttering the paper. Sometimes less is more."
15. Side example inclusion test: ~1 page and uses many of the paper's lemmas → include at the end. Lengthy technical calculation using the main theorem as a black box → separate follow-up paper. Many long examples → publish separately. Doesn't stand alone → arXiv appendix only, never journal.
16. Story-within-a-story (two-field papers): write the whole paper as if the second story doesn't exist; insert one self-contained section on connections/applications to the second field AFTER main results are stated, BEFORE the proofs. Same placement for "in a special case our proof simplifies" secondary stories — accept the small redundancy; the self-contained simple proof prepares the reader for the main proof.
17. Never "story within a story within a story" (Cloud Atlas). Two papers for two fields is rare and "ethically dubious unless both papers have some extra results the other does not, and you discuss the existence of the other paper straight in the introduction of each."
18. Practical: find your story by reading the stories of papers citing the same problem (GoogleScholar → MathSciNet → keywords/MSC); side benefit — you learn the state of the art.
