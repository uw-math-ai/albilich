# SU — Francis Su, "Guidelines for Good Mathematical Writing" (fetched 2026-07-08, PDF read in full, 4pp, dated Jan 19 2011)

## §1 Basics
1. Know your audience — the most important consideration. What background can we assume? What terminology to define? What voice: casual/professional, serious/inviting, terse/loquacious? (Homework default: write to another student in the course who hasn't done the assignment; remind readers of recently learned items: "Recall that a rational number can be expressed as a fraction.")
2. Set an invitational tone: present tense, "we" not "I" ("we construct a tangent plane…"), gentle commands ("let n be…", "recall that…", "consider the set of…").
3. Use complete sentences. All mathematics is written in complete sentences; displayed equations carry punctuation showing where they fit in the larger sentence. (Worked bad example: a wall of unconnected computations — "The reader should not have to figure out what the writer was thinking!"; the good version states the Problem, marks assumptions vs results, ends one display with a period and another with a comma, and answers the original question.)
4. Avoid shorthand in formal writing: write out "for all", "there exists", "implies", "if and only if", "such that" (no ∀ ∃ ⇒ ⇔ iff s.t.). NUANCE: ∈ is traditionally acceptable in formal writing, as are relations (<, >, ×, +, ∪, ∩), variable names, and set symbols (Q, R, Z).
5. Etiquette: avoid beginning sentences or phrases with a number or symbol ("41 people told me not to do it because it looks bad"); emphasize (italicize/underline) unfamiliar words about to be defined; learn other norms by observing your area's texts.

## §2 Towards elegance
6. Decide what's important to say: enough detail to highlight the most interesting/unexpected parts; "your primary role is to give insight," not to communicate all details. Don't bore readers with trivial algebra; show the interesting steps.
7. Highlight structure: summarize the outline of a long argument at the start and show progress through it; paragraphs group related ideas; "imagine a reader skimming your writing and only reading the first sentences" — they should see the flow; display only the most important equations; replace an oft-repeated argument by a lemma.
8. Choose good examples: simple enough to follow, interesting enough to retain the salient features; precede a very general proof with a specific-context example; consider a running example.
9. Avoid red herrings: omit details with no bearing on the solution ("m/n with no common factors" promises a later use; if never used, cut it).
10. Step back and simplify: after writing, ask what can be streamlined; did you use every tool you pulled out? (Worked example: a fake proof by contradiction whose contradictory hypothesis is never used — strip first and last sentences to get a direct proof.)
11. Refine, refine, refine: successive approximations; revisit suboptimal notation or definitions instead of leaving them ("a lazy writer would leave things as they are").

## §3 Finally
12. Observe the culture: norms are era- and community-dependent; calibrate against highly regarded texts near your document.
13. Acknowledge support received.
