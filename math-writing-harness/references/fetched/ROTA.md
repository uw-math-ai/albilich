# ROTA — Gian-Carlo Rota, "Ten Lessons I Wish I Had Been Taught" (fetched 2026-07-08, Notices AMS 44(1) 1997, read in full, 4pp)

Mostly a talk about lecturing and mathematical life; the writing-relevant lessons are extracted below. Lessons on blackboard technique, old age, and the Feynman method omitted as out of scope for a writing rubric.

## Writing-relevant lessons

1. **Every lecture (unit of exposition) should make only one main point** — "any philosopher who uses the word 'and' too often cannot be a good philosopher" (Hegel, endorsed). State one main point and repeat it like a theme with variations; make several points and "the cows will scatter all over the field." → For papers: each section/paper should have one identifiable main point (L3).
2. **Give them something to take home** — the audience/reader must leave with a stated, portable takeaway (Struik's "Give us something to take home!"). → Intro and conclusion must state the takeaway explicitly (L3).
3. **Publish the same result several times / present it in several versions** — the community is split into small groups with their own customs, notation, terminology; a result inaccessible to a group will be rediscovered and claimed by them. Riesz's thrice-published papers "gave the reader a feeling of definitiveness." → Exposition should be self-contained for its stated audience; translate notation for the venue (L3).
4. **You are more likely to be remembered by your expository work** — Hilbert (Zahlbericht, Foundations of Geometry, integral equations text), Feller (probability treatise). Expository value is real mathematical value; do not treat exposition as an afterthought (framing/motivation).
5. **Write informative introductions** — "Nowadays reading a mathematics paper from top to bottom is a rare event. If we wish our paper to be read, we had better provide our prospective readers with strong motivation to do so. A lengthy introduction, summarizing the history of the subject, giving everybody his due, and perhaps enticingly outlining the content of the paper in a discursive manner, will go some of the way towards getting us a couple of readers." As editor of Advances in Mathematics he routinely sent papers back to have their introductions LENGTHENED. → L3: introduction must summarize history, credit predecessors, and outline the content discursively.
6. **Give lavish acknowledgments** — cite and credit generously; readers who feel uncredited are alienated (his experiment: even irrelevant citations produced grateful letters — stated humorously, but the operative rule is: never under-credit). → L1/L5 citation practice: err on the side of crediting.
7. **Do not worry about your mistakes (two kinds of mistakes)** — fatal mistakes destroy a theory; contingent ones test its stability (Hilbert's collected papers: all but one error repairable without changing theorem statements). → Perspective for revision loops: distinguish fatal from contingent errors when triaging (L1 severity triage).

## Quotable calibration lines
- "The advice we give others is the advice that we ourselves need."
- "An audience is like a herd of cows, moving slowly in the direction they are being driven towards."
