# AARONSON — Scott Aaronson, "Ten Signs a Claimed Mathematical Breakthrough Is Wrong" (fetched 2026-07-08, https://scottaaronson.blog/?p=304)

The ten signs (verbatim):
1. "The authors don't use TeX."
2. "The authors don't understand the question."
3. "The approach seems to yield something much stronger and maybe even false
   (but the authors never discuss that)."
4. "The approach conflicts with a known impossibility result (which the authors
   never mention)."
5. "The authors themselves switch to weasel words by the end."
6. "The paper jumps into technicalities without presenting a new idea."
7. "The paper doesn't build on (or in some cases even refer to) any previous work."
8. "The paper wastes lots of space on standard material."
9. "The paper waxes poetic about 'practical consequences,' 'deep philosophical
   implications,' etc."
10. "The techniques just seem too wimpy for the problem at hand."

Qualifications: heuristics, not definitive tests; failing signs doesn't guarantee
wrongness and passing all ten doesn't guarantee correctness; known false positives
exist (Deutsch, Grover on TeX). Signs 6-10 are the most decisive; sign 10 is "the
slipperiest and hardest to apply — but also the decisive one in many cases."

Rubric use: fast Referee triage BEFORE deep proof checking; signs 3-4 are the
"proves too much / contradicts impossibility" global-error checks; 5 is the
weasel-word drift check (abstract vs conclusion claim strength); 6 is the
no-new-idea check; 7 related-work grounding; 8 standard-material padding; 9
overselling rhetoric; 10 techniques-vs-problem plausibility flag (heighten
scrutiny, never auto-reject).
