# MILNE — J.S. Milne, "Tips for Authors" (fetched 2026-07-08, https://www.jmilne.org/math/tips.html)

Sardonic anti-advice; every tip inverts into a critic CHECK.

Opening frame (verbatim): "If you write clearly, then your readers may understand
your mathematics and conclude that it isn't profound."

The tips (near-verbatim):
1. Never explain conditions or purpose. Begin with notation pages sans explanation;
   never clarify what you're proving or why; readers shouldn't discover results
   until the end, if at all.
2. Obscure definitions and sign conventions. Reference obscure papers for
   nonstandard definitions or omit explanations entirely; never explain sign
   conventions (prevents readers from catching errors).
3. Use "variation of definition": implicitly employ multiple definitions for a
   single term within one proof when struggling with a theorem.
4. Reverse notation conventions: use c, a, b to denote elements of sets A, B, C.
5. Conceal nontrivial results used in proofs; don't state them or give references;
   hide that you're using important theorems.
6. Provide incomplete references: never specify where results appear in cited
   works; alternatively cite weaker earlier papers instead of correct sources.
7. Number theorems, propositions, corollaries, definitions, remarks on separate
   schemes so readers abandon tracking references.
8. Muddle logical structure and quantifiers: write "A==>B==>C" ambiguously; use
   "If A, B, C" without clarity; always confuse quantifier ordering.
9. Begin/end sentences with symbols; place periods near mathematical symbols;
   attach footnote superscripts to symbols rather than words.
10. Embrace imprecision: use "so that" for "such that", "which" for "that";
    prefer the ambiguous over the precise.
11. Write in languages most mathematicians cannot read.

Inversion notes (for the rubric): each tip inverts to a positive CHECK —
motivated conditions; early accurate statement of results; in-paper or precisely
cited definitions; stable single definition per term; natural/parallel notation;
stated + referenced nontrivial inputs; precise references with locations; one
coherent numbering scheme; explicit logical structure and quantifier order; no
sentence-initial/terminal symbol collisions; no footnotes on symbols; such-that/
which-that precision; audience-readable language.
