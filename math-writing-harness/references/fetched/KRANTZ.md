## Foundations: something to say, someone to say it to

1. Have something to say, know what it is, know why you are saying it, and know to whom you are saying it — "keep that audience always in mind." Krantz calls a strong sense of audience "the single most important attribute of an effective writer."
2. Keep a specific, actually-known person in mind as the reader (same as Halmos §3); this predicts what questions will be asked, what confusions will arise, and which details need explaining.
3. Do not write "a love letter to yourself": a paper written primarily to convince the author his theorem is correct "will exhibit both passion and fervor, but it will tend to exclude the rest of the world." Think of the reader's convenience and understanding, not your own.
4. Do not write papers "that look like a letter home to Mom: they just start to write … never formally stating a theorem or even a definition, never coming to any particular point."
5. Know when to stop: "Stop when you have said what you have to say. Say it clearly, say it completely, say it forcefully, say it without leap or lacuna, but then shut up. To prattle on and on is not to convince further."
6. Written discourse is linear but thought is not; handle the mismatch explicitly with announced digressions ("now I will digress for a moment"), parentheses, or footnotes — do not leave the reader to untangle nonlinear order silently.
7. Rosalie Stemer's hallmark test: good writing "answers more questions than it raises."

## Proofreading, sound, and sense

8. Proofread in separate dedicated passes, one each for: (a) spelling/simple syntax, (b) accuracy, (c) organization and logic, (d) sense and flow of ideas, (e) sound, (f) overall coherence. (Halmos "never published a word before he had read it six times.")
9. Read your work aloud. "All the best writers whom I know read their work aloud to themselves." Reading aloud catches word-order obscurity that silent proofreading misses, because when proofreading your own work "you tend to supply meaning that is not actually present in the writing."
10. Prefer context-rich statements to terse ones. Bad: "The conjecture of Gauss (1830) is false." Good: "The lemmas of Euler (1766) and the example of Abel (1827) led Gauss to conjecture (1830) that all semistable curves are modular. The conjecture was widely believed, and more than fifty papers were written … To everyone's surprise and dismay, a counterexample was produced by Frobenius in 1902. This counterexample opened many doors." — "There is a tradition in written mathematics to conform to the terse. In your own writing, consider instead the advantages of telling the reader what is going on."
11. Do not repeat the same descriptive word, or even the same sound, in two consecutive sentences (nouns under discussion and "the" excepted).
12. Avoid intrusive alliteration. Bad: "This semisimple, sesquilinear operator serves to show sometimes that subgroups of S are sequenced." Good: "Observe that this operator is both semisimple and sesquilinear. These properties can lead to the conclusion that if G is a subgroup of S then G is sequenced."
13. Kill "very." Bad: "This is a very important operator, that has very specific properties, culminating in a very significant theorem." Good: "This operator will be significant for our studies. Its spectral properties, together with the fact that it is smoothing of order one, will lead to our first fundamental theorem." Rule of thumb (via [KnLR]): never use "very" unless you would be comfortable using "damn" in its place.
14. Choose terminology as if it will last: "the word 'continuous' is a perfect name for a certain type of function; the alternative terminology 'non-hypererratic' would be much less useful." Names change how objects are perceived even if not their properties.
15. Do not agonize over each word in a first draft — "Just get the ideas down on the page. But do agonize a bit when you are editing and proofreading."

## Sentences: simple, declarative, active

16. Use simple declarative sentences; break up compound sentences; "avoid run-on sentences at all cost." Mathematics is already logically complex — the connecting prose should not be.
17. Bad: "As we let x become closer and closer to 0, then y tends ever closer to t0." Good: "When x is close to 0 then y is close to t0." (Often better still: the notation lim_{x→0} y = t0.)
18. Restructure hypothesis-pileups into a "Let … Let … Suppose … Then" sequence. Bad: "If g is positive, f is continuous, the domain of f is open, and we further invoke Lemma 2.3.6, then the set of points at which f·g is differentiable is a set of the second category, provided that the space of definition of f is metrizable and separable." Good: "Let X be a separable metric space. Let f be a continuous function that is defined on an open subset of X. Suppose that g is any positive function. Define S to be the set of points x such that f·g is differentiable at x. Then, by Lemma 2.3.6, S is of second category." Vary "let" with "suppose" and "define."
19. Keep a clutch of alternatives for "therefore" ("thus," "so," "it follows that," "as a result"); a paragraph where every sentence begins with the same connective is uncomfortable to read.
20. Prefer active voice — it identifies the agent and uses fewer words. Bad: "The manifold M is acted upon by the Lie group G as follows:" Good: "The Lie group G acts on the manifold M as follows:" BUT (softer than Strunk–White/Dupré dogma) passive is legitimate when you have reason to stress the acted-upon object (the manifold over the group) — Krantz cites Lincoln's "Fourscore and seven years ago our fathers brought forth…" as passive-paced prose a strict active-voice edit would ruin.
21. Rethinking beats rewording. Bad: "It follows that the set Z will have no element of the set Y lying in it." (15 words) Better: "Therefore no element of Y lies in Z." Better still: "The sets Y and Z are disjoint." or "Therefore Y ∩ Z = ∅." Reaching the succinct version "required not only restating the proposition, but also thinking about its meaning."
22. When a sentence is not working, do not wrestle with it — reformulate it from scratch. (Churchill's "That is the sort of behavior up with which I will not put" → "I will not tolerate that sort of behavior.")
23. Write for a reader on Halmos's "walk in the woods," not one sitting in a library: if the reader must run to the stacks or Google "at every other sentence, then you are making the job too hard." Supply enough detail and heuristic that an unsure reader can "suspend his disbelief and move on."
24. Use short paragraphs, each with a simple point. Never make the crux of a main proof "a single, dense paragraph that is two pages long" — break the ideas into palatable bites.
25. Interweave prose and calculation: several consecutive pages of dense prose, or of tedious calculation, are both unappealing. Provide "comfortable stopping places on every page."

## Notation and symbols in prose

26. Never begin a sentence or clause with a symbol. Bad: "Let f be a function. f is said to be semicontinuous if …" Good: "A function f is said to be semicontinuous if …" Bad: "For most points x, x ∈ S." Good: "We see that x ∈ S for most points x."
27. Relational symbols are binary connectives, usable only in the exact form A < B. Bad: "Every real, nonsquare x < 0 …" — on first reading it is ambiguous between "every real nonsquare x is negative" and "every real nonsquare x that is less than zero…". If the connective phrase is not exactly what your sentence needs, write words.
28. Do not write logic-symbol streams in prose. Bad: "∀x∃y, x ≥ 0 ⇒ y² = x" Good: "Every nonnegative real number has a square root." (Same conclusion as Halmos §14.)
29. Introduce no notation you will not use. Mary Ellen Rudin's parody: "Let X be a set. Call it Y." Bad: "Let X be a compact metric subspace of the space Y. If f is a continuous, R-valued function on that space then it assumes both a maximum and a minimum value." (names X, Y, f, R and uses none). Best: "A continuous, real-valued function on a compact metric space assumes both a maximum value and a minimum value." — no notation, succinct and clear.
30. Plan notation in advance; keep it consistent from Section 1 to Section 10. Do not pull "For convenience we now change notation." Avoid local contradictions (shifting the free variable from x to y mid-argument), and try not to use one character for two purposes (the complex analyst's i as both √−1 and summation index: use j or k for indices).
31. Sentences containing symbols must still read as complete English aloud: "Since A < B we know that …" reads "Since A is less than B we know that …". Bad: "Look at this here equation: xⁿ + yⁿ = zⁿ."
32. Display long formulas and all important formulas; number/label only those referred to later. Do not display every formula (cumbersome) nor in-line every formula (tedious). Do not number every paragraph or every equation: "he will no longer be able to discern what is important and what is less so."
33. Number all displayed items (theorems, lemmas, corollaries, definitions) in a single sequence — Theorem 1.2, Corollary 1.3, Definition 1.4 — so the reader on the page with Lemma 1.6 knows where Theorem 1.5 is. Krantz explicitly calls the LaTeX default of separate counters per type "maddening; for the upshot is that I can never find anything." (Disagrees with LaTeX's out-of-the-box behavior.)
34. Notation precepts: "(1) Do not create new notation if there already exists well-known notation that is suitable for the job at hand; (2) If you must introduce new notation, then think about it carefully; (3) Strive for simplicity and clarity at all times." Fiddle with several candidate notations before committing; test on a colleague or student.
35. Standard notation is quasi-mandatory: a published paper defined Q17_reg for the bounded holomorphic functions on the disc when H∞(D) is universal — "just crazy," unless the author is embedding it in a new scale of spaces (then say so explicitly, as with Triebel–Lizorkin spaces subsuming Lp/Sobolev/Hardy).
36. When first defining a term, italicize the word or phrase being defined. When mentioning a word as an object, put it in quotation marks (Quine: "'Boston' has six letters. However Boston has 6 million people and no letters.").

## Mathematical idiom: if/then, iff, given, quantifiers

37. Never drop "then" after "if." Bad: "If x > 4, y < 2, the circle has radius at least 6, the sky is blue, the circle can be squared." Good: "… and the sky is blue, then the circle can be squared." Better: "Suppose that x > 4, … and the sky is blue. Then the circle can be squared." The reader can usually infer the missing "then," "But the reader should not have to do so."
38. Do not write "If f is a continuous function, then prove X." The hypothesis is part of what is to be proved: "Prove that, if f is a continuous function, then X."
39. Never begin a sentence with "if and only if." Bad: "If and only if x is nonnegative, can we be sure that the real number x has a real square root." Good: "A real number x has a real square root if and only if x ≥ 0."
40. "iff" (popularized by Halmos) is "a useful bridge between the formality of 'if and only if' and the convenience of 'if.'" Krantz endorses it — more permissive than guides that ban it from formal prose.
41. In definitions, custom writes "if" where logic demands "if and only if" ("we do not write what we mean"). Nobody will punish "if and only if," but "it is usually best to follow mathematical custom and simply to write 'if'" — or use "iff" as the compromise.
42. Avoid "given" as a filler transcribed from speech. Bad: "Given a metric space X, and a point p ∈ X, we see that …" Good: "If X is a metric space and p ∈ X, then …" (Matches Halmos §13 on empty "given.")
43. Never use "any" when "all"/"each"/"every" is meant — "any" can read existentially. Krantz's worked example: "Show that any continuous function f on [0,1] has a point M…" invites the student answer "The function f(x) = −(x − 1/2)² is continuous on [0,1] and the point M = 1/2 satisfies the conclusion." (Identical doctrine to Halmos §14.)
44. Quantify in the singular. Bad: "All continuous functions have a maximum." (suggests one shared maximum) Good: "Every continuous function has a maximum," or most precisely "Each continuous function has a maximum."
45. Prefer singular constructions generally. Bad: "Domains with noncompact automorphism groups have orbit accumulation points in their boundaries." (one group per domain or shared? several points or one?) Good: "A domain with noncompact automorphism group has an orbit accumulation point in its boundary."
46. Do not tack definitions on with a trailing "where." Bad: "Every convex polynomial function is of even degree, where we define a function to be convex if …" — "That is sloppy writing and there is no excuse for it: before you use a term, define it." (Same as Halmos's "where is a lazy afterthought.")
47. "We need only show that…" and "It suffices to show that…" are lovely turns of phrase; do not mangle them into "We only need to show that…" or "Suffices it to show that…," and do not overuse them.
48. Commas mark logical pauses, not breathing. Bad: "We went to the store, to buy some potatoes." Bad: "Now that we have our hypotheses in place, we state our theorem, with the point in mind, that we wish to understand the continuity, of functions in the class S."
49. Reserve "denote" for a specific symbol standing for a specific thing (test: excising A and substituting B preserves meaning exactly). "Let f be a continuous function" is correct; "Let f denote a continuous function" is not (f is an arbitrary function, not a name for one). Never "Denote X the set of all left-handed polyglots."

## Voice, tone, pretension

50. Default to "we" (the modern custom; participatory, unobtrusive). "One" traps the writer in awkward structures; "I" is irritating — except for genuinely personal statements, e.g. "At this time I do not know how to prove Conjecture A," where "we" or "one" would be affected and misleading. With craftsmanship you can often avoid first person entirely: "This completes the proof," "The next task is the proof of Lemma 4." (Krantz's default-"we" is more permissive than Halmos's preference for neutral declaratives.)
51. Avoid big words where small ones do; write simple, straightforward English partly because many readers are not native speakers. "To indulge in hippopotomonstrosesquipedalian tergiversation is not to show your erudition; rather, it is to be superficial."
52. Use foreign words and phrases sparingly; set them in italics (naturalized ones like "etc." in roman).
53. No contractions, no informal abbreviations, no slang, in formal writing. Define every acronym parenthetically at first use ("The SALT (Strategic Arms Limitation Talks) were progressing poorly…"); do not assume even "AMS" is known to all readers.
54. No flippancy or sarcasm ("we leave the details of this proof for the mentally infirmed" — published, regretted): "ten years hence, you will wish fervently that you had not included such phrases."
55. Avoid faddish prose and archaic modes of expression alike — both have a short shelf life ("in ten years the words will have no meaning").
56. Do not propagate the stock phrases and tiresome clichés of your subfield, nor slangy idioms ("cook up a function") that non-native speakers will emulate; overused words to shun: "very," "most," "nice," "interesting."
57. Suit tone to subject: "In mathematics, simpler is usually better. Flamboyant writing is out of place." ("The dwarf held a gewgaw" beats the Harlequin version.)
58. Custom overrides precision in established names: Zygmund's "World Series should be the 'World Sequence'" — correct and useless. Bear this in mind before inventing replacement terminology or notation.

## Grammar, syntax, and usage catalog (§1.11–1.12 highlights)

59. Say what you mean — subjects must match verbs, referents must refer, participles must not dangle. Bad: "As a valued customer of XYZ Co., your call is very important to us." Good: "Because you are a valued customer of XYZ Co., your call is very important to us." Bad: "Having spoken at hundreds of universities, the brontosaurus was a large green lizard."
60. Brevity: Bad: "In point of fact, we devolved upon the decision to solicit opinions, form an enumeration, and produce a tally." (19 words) Good: "We decided to take a vote." (6 words). Elevator-sign version: "Do not carry lighted tobacco products in the elevator." → "No smoking." Put every word under the microscope: what does it add?
61. Latin abbreviations: "cf." means "compare," not "see"; "e.g." = for example; "i.e." = that is; do not interchange them. "It is difficult to make a compelling case for 'i.e.' in favor of 'that is'" — when unsure, use the English.
62. "A comprises B": "The standing committee comprises two women, three men, and a donkey." Never "is comprised of" (use "is composed of").
63. Use the serial comma: "the good, the bad, and the ugly" — without it the reader may infer that "bad" and "ugly" are linked.
64. "Obviously/Clearly/Trivially": "In the best of circumstances, when you use these phrases you are endeavoring to push the reader around. In the worst of circumstances you are throwing up a smoke screen for something that you yourself have not thought through." Weed them out in proofreading; "it would be embarrassing to count the number of major published mathematical errors that have been prefaced with 'Obviously.'"
65. Foreign plurals: maximum/maxima, datum/data, criterion/criteria — do not use plural forms as singular.
66. Possessives: singular nouns always take 's ("Stokes's theorem," "Bliss's book"); plural nouns take bare apostrophe ("the students' confusion"); plurals of acronyms and decades take NO apostrophe ("TLAs," "1960s").
67. Subject–verb agreement across prepositional phrases: "The set of all morphisms is compact" (subject: set). Test by omitting the phrase. But "the sequence z_n converges to p" while "the numbers z_n converge to p" — both correct.
68. Do not use "this/that/these/those" without a clear referent. Bad: "A triangle is a three-sided polygon. This means that …" ("'this' what?") Folland's example: "Saddam Hussein was determined to resist attempts to force Iraqi troops out of Kuwait, although George Bush made it clear that he did not want to be seen as a wimp. This caused the Gulf War." — which clause caused it depends on your politics. Default: repeat the noun rather than use a vague pronoun.
69. who/whom, I/me: test compound constructions by breaking them into component sentences. Bad: "The pastor, whom expected a large donation, smiled warmly." Bad: "Him and me proved the isotopy isomorphism theorem in 1967."
70. if vs. whether (Piranian's example): "Go to the window and see whether it is raining; if it is raining, then let Fido inside."
71. imply/infer: assumptions imply a conclusion; one infers the conclusion from the assumptions.
72. less/fewer: "fewer" compares numbers (discrete sets), "less" compares quantity (continua) — "Ten Items or Fewer." In math "3 is less than 5" is right; avoid "3 is smaller than 5" ("smaller" is about size — perhaps the digit).
73. lay/lie: "lay" transitive, "lie" intransitive; past tense of "lie" is "lay" ("Yesterday I … laid down my books and then I lay down").
74. its/it's: "it's" only for "it is"; no apostrophe in any possessive pronoun (its, hers, theirs).
75. hopefully: an adverb modifying a verb ("she looked at him hopefully"), not a sentence adverb. Bad: "Hopefully the weather will be better today." Good: "I hope that the weather is better today."
76. that/which: "that" restricts, "which" amplifies. Worked mathematical example: "A holomorphic function that vanishes on S must be identically zero" is restrictive, so "that" is correct — though Krantz concedes sound-and-sense sometimes justifies the weightier "which."
77. Participles: Bad: "Shining like the sun, the man gazed happily upon the heap of gold coins." Good: "The man gazed happily upon the heap of gold coins, which shone like the sun." Boas's maxim: "When dangling, don't use participles."
78. Prepositions at sentence end: usually the errant preposition is a spare word — cut it ("Where do we stop playing at?" → "When do we stop playing?"). Boas: "Watch out for prepositions that sentences end with." But do not contort (Churchill, rule 22): sound and sense may overrule.
79. Split infinitives: generally avoid, but not at the cost of precision — Folland's counterexample: "Hence we are forced to severely restrict the allowable range of values of the variable x" (where else can "severely" go?).
80. Redundancy in phrase: avoid "old adage," "refer back," "advance planning," "past history," "strangled to death" — "an ignorant and superfluous use of adjectives." (Logical redundancy as a teaching device is separately GOOD — rule 92.)
81. shall/will (Strunk–White via Krantz): first person futurity "shall," determination "will"; reversed for second/third person. "Bather in Distress: 'I shall drown and no one will save me.' Suicide: 'I will drown and no one shall save me.'"
82. American quotation convention: commas and periods inside closing quotes, colons and semicolons outside; be consistent and be ready to arm-wrestle the copy editor.
83. Hyphen vs. en dash for joint names: "the Riemann–Lebesgue lemma" (en dash — two people), not "Riemann-Lebesgue" (hyphen suggests one hyphenated person); copy editors will enforce this.
84. Numbers in words vs. numerals: sources conflict (cutoff at 20 or 101) — "select a standard and be consistent."
85. Word order changes meaning; as calibration, insert "only" in every position of "I helped Carl prove quadratic reciprocity last week" and watch the meaning change.
86. No rule of grammar is etched in stone: when the strictly-correct sentence "sounds just awful," override the rule — but knowingly, by ear ("sound and sense"), not by ignorance.

## Organizing a paper

87. Title must convey information: "On a theorem of Hoofnagel says almost nothing"; "Quadratic convergence of Lax/Wendroff schemes with optimal estimates on the error term is ideal" — it says what the paper is about and what point it makes.
88. Front matter checklist: title, authors (pick one name-form for life — indexing should be "a zero-one game: it should retrieve all your works or none of them"), affiliations, addresses/e-mail, date, abstract, key words, subject classification, grant thanks (thanks to funding agencies is mandatory).
89. Key words must be searchable content words: "'new,' 'interesting,' and 'optimal' are not good choices … 'pseudoconvex,' 'Cauchy problem,' and 'exotic cohomologies' are good choices."
90. Do NOT write the paper in strict logical order (Bourbaki/Hilbert order is for archiving, "this is not the way that we learn"). Structure: (§1) first paragraph or two summarize the main results in nontechnical language; then history of the problem, earlier results, and exactly what progress this paper represents; acknowledgements and an outline of the paper. (§2) technical definitions and formal statements of results. (§3) the "big steps" of the proof — the technical lemmas and how they fit together to yield the theorem. (End) the nasty details of the lemma proofs. Each additional epsilon of reader effort yields "additional and predictable benefit."
91. Write the introduction for the referee: "You cannot, indeed you must not, assume that the referee will compensate for your shortcomings. If you do not explain what the paper is about, why you wrote it, why your theorems are new … then nobody else is going to do it for you," and the referee "will conclude quickly that this is just another piece of second rate drivel and will reject it."
92. Redundancy is a kindness: recall a definition or theorem the reader last saw 50 pages ago — quick recap or at least a cross-reference. "Think of how much you would appreciate this assist if you were the reader."
93. One journal at a time — "the one hard and fast rule in this business." (Books, by contrast, may be submitted to several publishers simultaneously.)
94. Collaboration: settle authorship questions at the outset; "alphabetical is almost always best" for name order; touch base courteously about follow-on papers that build on joint work.

## Theorems, proofs, definitions

95. Hold a theorem statement under ten lines, preferably five (some guides say one sentence). Never enumerate 25 hypotheses: "you never need to state a theorem in this tedious manner." Technique: pre-package hypothesis clusters into named definitions ahead of the statement — define "regular," "amenable," "smooth" beforehand, then: "Theorem: If f is a regular, amenable, smooth function, then it operates on L(H) in the sense of the functional calculus." One sentence, two lines; the reader carries away the three-part shape of the hypotheses. This is "not just … sleight of hand; in fact we are providing organization and context."
96. A proof of more than a few pages must be broken into lemmas and corollaries "organized in such a fashion that the reader can always tell where he has been and where he is going." Push technicalities toward the end; provide signposts.
97. The Claim device: state "We claim that the following is true," then "Assuming this claim for the moment, we complete the proof." It says "This is the crux of the proof, but its verification involves some nasty details. Trust me on this for the moment, and let me show you how the crux leads to a happy ending."
98. The deferred lemma: enunciate a technical lemma at the point of use with "Proof deferred to Section 8" — but then label parts of the paper well; "Do not leave your poor reader with a head full of dangling claims and unproved lemmas to sort out." Gillman's rule (endorsed): "be sure that your reader always knows the status of every statement that you make." (Same doctrine as Halmos's status-honesty §10.)
99. No hanging/afterthought theorems. Bad: rattling on for pages then "Note that we have proved the following theorem: Theorem [The Riemann Mapping Theorem]: …" — "What a disservice to the reader … The offhand 'Note by the way that we have proved the Riemann mapping theorem' is a real travesty." (Agrees with Halmos §11: state the theorem first.)
100. Proof by contradiction: never open with "Not." or "Deny." ("cute" but hinders communication). Open with "Seeking a contradiction, suppose that f is a continuous, real-valued function on a compact set K that does not assume a maximum. Then …"
101. Build definitions in stepping stones — function, then continuous function, then intermediate value property, then existence of √2 — "so that the reader is given a chance to internalize idea n before going on to idea (n+1)." Never spit out all the ideas in one sentence or paragraph.
102. Calibrate how much you define: do not define what every qualified graduate student knows (Hilbert space in a von Neumann algebra paper); do define nonstandard coinages. For standard terminology cite a well-known reference — and "do your reader the kindness" of citing the specific definition number or page, not just the book. If the best reference is a semi-obscure article, repeat the definition instead.
103. Introduce no more new terminology than necessary: "If your paper contains a plethora of unfamiliar language, then it may cause your reader to suspect that you actually have nothing to say."
104. Never state lemmas parenthetically mid-proof ("(We use here the fact that every pseudo-melange is complete. Proof: …)" — "Here the author is writing a love letter to himself"). Put every lemma and definition in place, with connecting material, before it is needed.

## Abstracts and bibliographies

105. The abstract (≤ ~10 lines) must be self-contained, with no bibliographic references and minimal notation/jargon. "Eschew nasty details": not the exact estimate with its ǫs and δs, but that you prove a new interior elliptic estimate in the Nikol'skii topology improving Nirenberg, with applications to free boundary problems.
106. Funnel arithmetic: "any reader who looks at your paper will read the abstract, only 20% of those will read the introduction, and perhaps one fourth of that 20% will dip into the body of the paper" — weight your polishing accordingly.
107. Bibliography from primary sources only: gaze upon the actual paper (cover/TOC data are frequently wrong; copied references propagate errors for decades, per Higham).
108. List only references cited in the text; sentimental or "might be handy" entries offer the reader nothing (use a separate "Additional Reading" list in a book if needed).
109. Complete data always: author, title, journal, volume, year, pages. The minimalist "1. Knuth, 1992." style is "irresponsible … In fifty years it would not mean anything to anyone."
110. Cite with precision: never bare "see Dunford and Schwartz" (2500+ pages, three volumes) — cite the specific theorem or page. If forced into "by a variant of the argument in this paper," be as specific as you can.
111. No bibliographic references in footnotes in mathematics — footnote tags can be mistaken for exponents.
112. Citation placement: Krantz prefers on-the-fly in-text citation over Steenrod's end-of-chapter bibliographic essays ("Princeton style"), explicitly disagreeing with Steenrod for books: the essay style "serves the big shots well" but overlooks small contributors, while in-text citation "systematically holds you accountable."

## Expository writing (surveys, opinion pieces, prefaces)

113. The survey reader sits on a park bench with coffee, not with "a pencil gripped in his fist." Do not prove the big theorems; describe ingredients, sketch structure, refer to the books. Do not assume the reader knows the jargon, the motivation, or has "the perfect background."
114. Survey proofs may honestly fudge: "State theorems precisely and correctly but, when presenting the proof, say 'For simplicity, we consider only a special case' or 'For a quick proof, let us assume that the function is actually real analytic.'" (E.g., inverse function theorem: hard for C¹, easy for C² via Taylor remainder.) A worked example can carry the proof: "This example is in fact the enemy. The proof shows that this example represents the only thing that could possibly go wrong, and then systematically shows that the hypotheses rule the example out."
115. A survey is a story with a climax and a stirring conclusion (a look back over the ground covered, historical turning points, speculation on future milestones) — "Simply to begin citing technical results in chronological sequence is not to write an effective survey."
116. A survey needs its own extensive bibliography — "Your list of references is your detailed definition of what the subject is" — and it is negligent to just point at three standard treatises.
117. Political duty of the surveyor: "Be absolutely certain that you have not slighted any of the players in the subject, nor inadvertently misrepresented their contributions"; circulate the survey to all key players before publication.
118. Opinion pieces: enunciate the thesis unmistakably in the first paragraph; support it with researched facts (anecdotes alone are "at first entertaining but, in the end, not convincing"; dry facts alone don't hold attention); close by summing the major points and forcing the conclusion. Anti-model, the midwestern-orator parody: "Agriculture is important. Our rivers are full of fish. The future lies ahead."
119. Every scholarly piece needs prefatory remarks answering: What material is covered? At what level? For whom? What are the prerequisites? What need does this fill? "They keep you honest, and keep you on your course."
120. Write the Preface twice: a version before you begin (to organize and orient yourself) and again when finished ("then you know in detail what you have written"). Even a Preface written and shredded "would have been an important and worthwhile exercise."

## Books: planning and revision

121. Plan the whole book before writing: detailed outline, chapter headings then section headings; carry the outline "emblazoned on your frontal lobes." "After writing many books, I can say with some confidence that the planned approach is far superior" to letting events dictate.
122. The Table of Contents is the acid test: "If you find yourself unable to write the TOC, then maybe God is trying to tell you something … What makes you think that you will be able to write it after having written all the chapters if you cannot write it before?" Make it as detailed as you can; you are not wedded to it.
123. Write with tunnel vision after planning: pick one section, write it; note stray thoughts about other chapters in a file rather than jumping around — "Bouncing around from section to section … chasing corrections around a never-ending vortex—is a sure path to disillusionment, depression, and ultimate failure."
124. On Halmos's spiral method (write 1, 2, rewrite 1, write 3, rewrite 1–2, …): Krantz endorses reviewing Chapters 1..(n−1) after writing Chapter n, but flags the side effect Halmos doesn't: "Chapter 1 of your book will receive more attention than any other part … As a result, your book could appear to the reader to become looser and looser as it proceeds." Use the method with awareness of this bias. (Explicit qualification of Halmos.)
125. Rewrite, don't patch: "only wimps revise their manuscripts; great authors throw their work in the trash and rewrite. … Treat your first try as just getting the words out … you may as well discard it (and don't peek!)." Apply selectively — a month-long proof of a hard proposition does not go in the trash.
126. The classroom path: develop the book from lecture notes taught several times — classroom-tested, colleague-criticized, abandonable without loss of face.
127. Use appendices for calculations or lemma-clusters that must be included but would interrupt the flow ("in order not to interrupt the train of thought, we include details in Appendix III"); also for background and ancillary results.
128. Index by concept, not by word; the author, not a professional indexer, should build it ("nobody knows your book better than you"). "Many otherwise fine mathematics books are flawed by lack of an adequate index (or … an adequate bibliography)." Add a Table of Notation and Glossary where appropriate — they "can make the difference between an advanced tract accessible to just a few specialists, or a book that opens up a field."
129. Author-fatigue warning: many books lavish "enthusiasm and creativity and energy" on the first fifty pages and run out of steam; the reader suffers. Budget effort across the whole manuscript (see rule 124's bias in the opposite direction).

## Figures

130. Label every figure explicitly (e.g., "Chapter 3 Section 2 Figure 5") and make sure the text contains a specific reference to each figure by its label — "do not leave it to the reader to determine what figure goes with which set of ideas." Same for tables. Titles and captions help though are not mandatory.
131. "A good figure is not too busy, does not have extraneous information or extraneous pen-strokes, and displays its message prominently and clearly." (Krantz points to Tufte for depth.)
